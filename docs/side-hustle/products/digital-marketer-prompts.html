Chapter 011 ¬∑ SEO & Content Strategy

1.1 ‚Äî Keyword Cluster Builder

I'm building a content strategy for [brand] in the [industry] space. Take this seed list of keywords and group them into topical clusters based on search intent and semantic relevance. For each cluster provide: a pillar page topic, 3-5 supporting article ideas, dominant search intent (informational/commercial/transactional), and estimated content difficulty (low/medium/high).

Seed keywords: [paste 15-30 keywords]

Why It Works: Forces the AI to organise existing research into actionable content architecture rather than generating generic keyword ideas. The pillar-and-cluster output maps directly to site structure.

Cluster: Home Espresso Brewing ‚Äî Pillar: “The Complete Guide to Making Espresso at Home” | Supporting: best machines under ¬£500 (commercial), dialling in grind size (informational), espresso vs filter compared (informational) | Difficulty: Medium

1.2 ‚Äî Content Brief Generator

Write a content brief for "[article title]" targeting "[primary keyword]". Include: primary/secondary keywords with placement suggestions, search intent analysis, recommended word count and format, detailed H2/H3 outline, 3 unique angles to differentiate from current top-ranking content, internal linking suggestions (our site covers [topics]), and a meta title + description within character limits.

Why It Works: Replicates the brief a senior SEO strategist would write. The “unique angles” instruction prevents generic rehashing of page-one results.

Unique angle: Survey 3-5 Reddit threads for real-user complaints that competing articles ignore (wobble at max height, customer service times).

1.3 ‚Äî Competitor Content Gap Analysis

I run [brand] in the [industry] niche. My top 3 competitors are [URLs]. Identify: 5 topic areas they likely cover that I'm missing, 5 content formats I'm not using, and 3 "blue ocean" content opportunities with lower competition. For each gap, explain the commercial value and suggest a content piece.

Why It Works: Asking the AI to find gaps produces strategic recommendations. The “blue ocean” instruction pushes beyond obvious ideas.

Gap: “[Tool] vs [Competitor]” comparison pages capturing high-intent traffic ‚Äî these convert at 2-3x blog rates because readers are one step from purchase.

1.4 ‚Äî Topical Authority Map

Map a complete content ecosystem for "[broad topic]". Include: 1 core pillar (3,000+ words), 8-12 cluster topics (1,000-1,500 words each), internal linking structure with rationale, suggested publication order that builds authority progressively, and content types for each piece. Our audience is [describe].

Why It Works: Produces a full content roadmap with publication sequencing ‚Äî transforms a topic list into a genuine execution plan.

Phase 1: “What Is Revenue Operations?” ‚Üí “RevOps vs Sales Ops” ‚Üí “How to Structure a RevOps Team” (each links backward, building authority progressively)

1.5 ‚Äî Search Intent Classifier

Classify each keyword by search intent. For each provide: intent type (informational/commercial/transactional/navigational), confidence level, ideal content format, and one-sentence description of what the searcher wants.

Keywords: [one per line]

Why It Works: Intent classification determines whether you write a blog post or a landing page. This does in seconds what takes 30+ minutes with a spreadsheet.

“best CRM for small business” ‚Üí Commercial Investigation / High / Comparison listicle / Evaluating options before buying

1.6 ‚Äî Blog-to-Video Script Converter

Convert this blog post into a 90-second [YouTube Shorts / TikTok / Reels] script. Tone: [describe]. Structure: hook (first 3 seconds), setup (10-15 seconds), value delivery (2-3 punchy points), CTA. Include [on-screen text suggestions]. Max 15 words per sentence.

Blog post: [paste content]

Why It Works: Handles the creative work of format translation ‚Äî repurposing content across platforms without the mental overhead.

Hook: “Your email open rates are lying to you.” [TEXT: “STOP trusting open rates”]

1.7 ‚Äî SERP Feature Opportunity Finder

For "[target keyword]", analyse which SERP features likely appear (featured snippets, PAA, video carousels, etc.). For each: describe the content format needed to win it, provide a structural recommendation, and rate effort-to-impact.

Why It Works: Ranking #1 matters less than owning SERP real estate. This maps the specific moves to capture featured snippets and PAA boxes.

Featured Snippet: Write a 40-50 word definition directly under “What is [term]?” ‚Äî Google favours concise answers beginning with “[Term] is a‚Ä¶” | Effort: Low / Impact: High

Chapter 022 ¬∑ Email Marketing

2.1 ‚Äî Subject Line Generator

Generate 15 subject lines for: product/offer [describe], audience [describe], goal [opens/clicks/purchases], tone [urgent/playful/professional]. Tag each with its psychological trigger (curiosity gap, social proof, FOMO, specificity, personalisation, pattern interrupt). Group by trigger. All under 50 characters.

Why It Works: Tagging psychological triggers lets you A/B test mechanisms, not just words ‚Äî producing compounding learnings.

Curiosity Gap: “We almost didn’t send this” | Specificity: “23% more demos in 11 days”

2.2 ‚Äî Welcome Sequence Architect

Design a 5-email welcome sequence for [brand]. Subscriber source: [lead magnet/trial/newsletter]. For each email: send timing, 2 subject line options, purpose and emotional arc, body copy (150-200 words), primary + fallback CTA, and the psychological bridge to the next email. Move from trust-building to soft-sell by Email 5.

Why It Works: The “psychological bridge” ensures a coherent narrative instead of five disconnected broadcasts.

Email 3 bridge: End with “Tomorrow I’ll show you the exact process ‚Äî including the spreadsheet template we use internally.”

2.3 ‚Äî Re-Engagement Campaign

Write a 3-email win-back sequence for subscribers inactive [60/90/120] days. Tone progression: friendly check-in ‚Üí value reminder ‚Üí "should we part ways?" with clear unsubscribe. Under 100 words per email, single low-commitment CTA each.

Why It Works: The “part ways” instruction produces authentic copy that paradoxically increases re-engagement ‚Äî treating readers as adults works.

Email 3: “We’ll quietly remove you in 7 days. Either way, thanks for giving us a shot.”

2.4 ‚Äî A/B Test Copy Variants

Here's my control email: [paste subject, body, CTA]. Generate 3 variants, each changing ONE variable: Variant A ‚Äî different emotional angle, B ‚Äî different structure, C ‚Äî different CTA. For each, state: "This will outperform the control if [specific assumption]."

Why It Works: Single-variable constraint produces testable hypotheses. The “outperform if” statement turns every test into a learning opportunity regardless of result.

Variant B hypothesis: Will outperform if audience is suffering “listicle fatigue” and responds better to narrative.

2.5 ‚Äî Newsletter Content Planner

Create a 4-week newsletter plan for [brand], [frequency], audience [describe]. Pillars: [list 3]. For each edition: theme, subject line, 3-4 content sections (50 words each), one pattern interrupt (poll/hot take/challenge), and suggested word count.

Why It Works: A month of thematic variety upfront prevents newsletter burnout and ensures you balance education, entertainment, and promotion.

Week 3 ‚Äî “The Contrarian Edition”: Subject: “Unpopular opinion: your content calendar is holding you back”

2.6 ‚Äî Transactional Email Humaniser

Rewrite this automated email to match our brand voice while keeping all essential info. Add one moment of personality, ensure scannability, include a soft brand touchpoint. Voice: [describe].

Current email: [paste]

Why It Works: Transactional emails have the highest open rates yet most brands waste them on robotic boilerplate.

After: “Your order just left our warehouse. Track it here ‚Äî and our setup guide means you’ll be ready the moment it arrives.”

Chapter 033 ¬∑ Social Media

3.1 ‚Äî Platform Content Adaptor

Adapt this content for [LinkedIn/X/Instagram/TikTok]: [paste source]. Match platform conventions, optimise for algorithm signals, include a scroll-stopping first line, suggest posting time and format, add a conversation-starting question.

Why It Works: Forces reasoning about platform-specific algorithm mechanics ‚Äî producing native content, not cross-posts.

LinkedIn: “I spent ¬£12,000 on Google Ads. Here’s what the dashboard didn’t tell me: ‚Üì” [line break triggers ‘see more’]

3.2 ‚Äî Engagement Hook Generator

Generate 10 hooks for [platform] about [topic], each using a different technique: contrarian statement, specific statistic, "you're doing X wrong", before/after, knowledge gap question, bold prediction, relatable frustration, social proof, urgency, curiosity gap. First 2 lines only. Mark each as best for awareness/engagement/conversion.

Why It Works: Ten distinct psychological techniques create a diverse creative bank instead of ten angles on the same idea.

Before/After (conversion): “6 months ago: 20 hours/week on reporting. Last week: 45 minutes.”

3.3 ‚Äî Hashtag Strategy Builder

Build a hashtag strategy for [brand] on [platform], [follower count]. Provide: 5 high-volume, 10 mid-volume, 5 low-volume hashtags, 3 branded suggestions, recommended mix per post, and 3 hashtags to avoid (with reasons).

Why It Works: The tiered approach balances discoverability with targeting, and the “avoid” list prevents mistakes that quietly tank reach.

Avoid: #MarketingTips (oversaturated ‚Äî posts vanish instantly), #FollowForFollow (spam signal)

3.4 ‚Äî Community Response Framework

Create response templates for: positive review, valid complaint, angry comment, feature request, competitor comparison, spam/troll, media enquiry. Each needs: human-sounding template, principles, escalation criteria, response time target.

Why It Works: Community management at scale requires frameworks, not improvisation.

Competitor comparison: “Great question! [Competitor] does [strength] well. Where we focus differently is [differentiator].” Principle: never disparage ‚Äî confidence beats defensiveness.

3.5 ‚Äî Viral Content Framework

Design a [platform] content piece using framework [A: Myth vs Reality / B: "I analysed 100 things" / C: Starter Pack / D: Day in the Life]. Topic: [your topic]. Provide: full copy, visual direction, engagement drivers (saves/shares/comments), and a "reply bait" element.

Why It Works: Viral content follows structural patterns ‚Äî choosing a proven framework dramatically increases shareability.

Reply bait: “What’s the biggest marketing myth YOU still see? Drop it below üëá” ‚Äî works because people love correcting others.

3.6 ‚Äî Content Calendar Batch Creator

Create 20 posts for [brand] on [platform], 4 weeks. Pillars: educational, social proof, behind-the-scenes, engagement. Rotate evenly, vary formats, include 2 timely posts. Each: full caption + format + best posting day. No consecutive same-pillar posts.

Why It Works: Batching eliminates daily “what do I post?” paralysis. Rotation rules prevent defaulting to one content type.

Post 7: “Honest question: when a brand emails after your free trial, does it make you MORE or LESS likely to buy?” [poll]

3.7 ‚Äî LinkedIn Thought Leadership Writer

Write a LinkedIn post about [topic] as a [title] with [X years] experience. Bold opening insight, specific story or failure, 2-3 actionable takeaways, reflective question. No clich√©s ("thrilled to announce", "Agree?"). 150-200 words, line breaks every 1-2 sentences.

Why It Works: The anti-clich√© instruction and story requirement prevent generic hustle-culture content.

“Last year I killed our best-performing campaign. It drove 40% of leads. My team thought I’d lost the plot. But those leads converted at 2%…”

Chapter 044 ¬∑ Paid Advertising

4.1 ‚Äî Google Ads Copy Generator

Write 3 responsive search ad sets for "[keyword]". USPs: [list 3]. Each set: 15 headlines (‚â§30 chars, mix benefit/feature/CTA/proof), 4 descriptions (‚â§90 chars), pin recommendations. Include exact keyword in 3+ headlines. Include numbers in 2+ headlines.

Why It Works: Matches Google’s RSA structure exactly, including pin strategy and character limits.

Headline 8: “Trusted by 2,400+ UK Teams” (28 chars) ‚Äî Pin to Position 1 for B2B.

4.2 ‚Äî Meta Ads Creative Brief

Create a Meta ad brief: objective [awareness/consideration/conversion], product [describe], audience [describe], budget [amount]. Provide: 3 ad concepts (emotional/rational/social proof angles), each with primary text (‚â§125 words), headline, description, CTA button, visual direction, and audience targeting suggestions. Include a testing plan.

Why It Works: Three distinct angles test genuinely different approaches. Visual direction bridges the copywriter-designer gap.

Rational visual: Split-screen. Left: cluttered desk, chaos. Right: clean dashboard, coffee. Muted tones vs bright.

4.3 ‚Äî LinkedIn Audience Persona Builder

Build 3 audience personas for LinkedIn ads promoting [product] to [market]. Each: specific job titles (5-10), seniority and company size, industry verticals, daily pain points, success metrics, objections, LinkedIn targeting parameters, and tailored messaging angle.

Why It Works: Persona-specific campaigns match LinkedIn’s targeting granularity with message relevance.

“The Overwhelmed Ops Lead”: Spends 60% of the week on reports instead of strategy. Feels invisible until something breaks.

4.4 ‚Äî Landing Page Copy Generator

Write landing page copy for [offer]. Traffic source: [channel], visitor awareness level: [describe]. Include: hero section (headline, sub-head, CTA, proof), problem section (3 pains in customer language), solution section, 3-5 features with benefits, social proof structure, 3 objections with counters, final CTA (3 button text options). Paragraphs under 3 lines.

Why It Works: Traffic source context ensures copy matches the visitor’s mindset on arrival.

Objection counter: “Most tools give you dashboards. We give you answers. If it doesn’t click in the first week, we’ll rebuild it or refund you.”

4.5 ‚Äî A/B Test Hypothesis Bank

Generate 10 A/B test hypotheses for [page/campaign/sequence]. Each: element to test, control vs variant, hypothesis ("If we [change], then [metric] will [outcome] because [reasoning]"), expected impact, sample size needed. Prioritise by effort-to-impact ‚Äî quick wins first.

Why It Works: Most teams struggle with knowing what to test. This creates a prioritised experiment backlog.

Quick win: CTA “Get Started” ‚Üí “See My Results” ‚Äî outcome-oriented CTAs outperform action-oriented for consideration-stage visitors.

4.6 ‚Äî Ad Spend Audit

I'm spending [budget] across [platforms]. Metrics: [paste CTR, CPC, conversion rate, CPA, ROAS per campaign]. Identify: underperforming campaigns vs [industry] benchmarks, where spend is wasted, budget reallocation recommendations, 3 optimisations for this week, and metrics I'm likely NOT tracking.

Why It Works: Real numbers produce specific analysis. The “not tracking” question surfaces blind spots.

Alert: Brand term campaign at ¬£2.10 CPC with no competition. Pause, verify organic captures it, redirect ¬£600/month to your CPA-constrained campaign.

Chapter 055 ¬∑ Analytics & Reporting

5.1 ‚Äî Data Interpreter

Analyse this data as a senior marketing analyst: [paste metrics]. Provide: 3 most important trends (with numbers), what to double down on, what needs attention, 2 non-obvious insights, and recommended next actions.

Why It Works: “Non-obvious insights” pushes beyond restating data. Actions prevent analysis paralysis.

Non-obvious: Mobile bounce spiked 18% on Tuesdays ‚Äî cross-reference with email sends driving mobile traffic to non-mobile-optimised pages.

5.2 ‚Äî Executive Campaign Summary

Write a 250-word executive summary: campaign [name], duration [dates], budget [spend], results [metrics]. Lead with revenue impact, include one honest underperformance, end with "what's next". No jargon.

Why It Works: Execs scan summaries, not dashboards. Impact first, honesty included, action-oriented ending.

“The webinar funnel converted at 4% ‚Äî below our 12% benchmark ‚Äî suggesting topic-audience mismatch.”

5.3 ‚Äî Campaign Post-Mortem

Post-mortem for [campaign]. Goal: [KPIs], results: [metrics], budget: [vs planned]. Structure: what worked (specific tactics), what didn't (with WHY hypotheses), surprises, root causes (deeper than surface), action items with suggested owners.

Why It Works: The “root causes” instruction prevents stopping at symptoms.

Root cause: Ads promised “simple pricing” but the landing page led with a feature matrix. Expectation violated within 3 seconds.

5.4 ‚Äî Attribution Analysis Explainer

Channels: [list with spend]. Conversion data: [paste]. Explain: what last-touch tells and misses, what linear/time-decay would reveal, which channels are undervalued, a realistic attribution approach for our tooling, and 3 decisions to make differently.

Why It Works: Turns attribution confusion into a narrative explaining why your current model misleads.

Undervalued: Organic social shows zero last-touch conversions but builds trust over 3-4 impressions before the branded Google search that gets all the credit.

5.5 ‚Äî KPI Framework Designer

Design a KPI framework for [team] at a [company size] [industry] company. Primary objective: [describe]. Include: 3-5 north star metrics, supporting weekly metrics, vanity metrics to stop tracking (with reasons), reporting cadence, and alert thresholds.

Why It Works: Distinguishes signal from noise with built-in action triggers.

Drop: Follower count. A 2,000-follower account at 8% engagement outperforms 10,000 at 1%. Track engagement rate instead.

Chapter 066 ¬∑ Brand & Messaging

6.1 ‚Äî Brand Voice Guidelines

Create brand voice guidelines for [brand] targeting [audience]. Personality: [3 words]. Produce: 4 voice traits with "this, not that" examples, tone shifts across contexts (social/support/web/crisis), 10 always-use and 10 never-use words, grammar rules, and 3 sample paragraphs (homepage/tweet/support reply).

Why It Works: “This, not that” and specific word lists create guidelines a new team member can follow on day one.

‚úÖ “We’ve helped 200 teams streamline onboarding.” ‚ùå “We’re the #1 onboarding solution.” ‚Äî Specific proof vs empty superlatives.

6.2 ‚Äî Messaging Framework Builder

Build a messaging framework for [product] targeting [audience]. Include: one-line positioning (under 15 words), 30-second elevator pitch, 3 key messages with proof points, messaging per awareness stage (unaware/problem-aware/solution-aware/product-aware), and objections mapped to counter-messages.

Why It Works: Awareness-stage mapping prevents using conversion language on people who don’t yet understand the problem.

Problem-aware: “You’re spending 15 hours/week reconciling data. Every tool you’ve tried adds another dashboard to check.”

6.3 ‚Äî Competitor Differentiation Matrix

I compete with [3 competitors] in [market]. Create: feature comparison weighted by customer value, messaging comparison (what each leads with), gaps nobody's addressing, our "only we" statement, and positioning recommendation (1-2 word category to own).

Why It Works: The “only we” exercise forces genuine uniqueness rather than manufactured differentiation.

Gap: Every competitor talks about “saving time.” None addresses migration anxiety. Lead with “zero switching pain.”

6.4 ‚Äî Value Proposition Canvas

Refine our value proposition using Jobs-To-Be-Done. Customer: [describe]. Map: customer jobs (functional/social/emotional), pains, gains, our pain relievers, our gain creators, and a fit statement paragraph.

Why It Works: JTBD produces value props grounded in customer reality, separating pains from gains to address both benefits and anxieties.

Emotional job: Feel competent during board presentations. Current tools produce data dumps, not stories.

6.5 ‚Äî Tone-of-Voice Audit

Audit these content pieces for voice consistency against our intended brand: [paste 3-4 samples]. For each: rate 1-10, identify on-brand and off-brand phrases, suggest rewrites, note contradictions between pieces.

Why It Works: Turns subjective “does this sound like us?” into a structured, repeatable review.

Social post ‚Äî 4/10: “We’re absolutely thrilled!” reads as corporate excitement. Rewrite: “New feature, live today. Here’s what it does.”

Chapter 077 ¬∑ Growth & CRO

7.1 ‚Äî Conversion Funnel Analyser

Analyse our funnel: [list stages with numbers]. For each drop-off: conversion rate vs [industry] benchmarks, top 3 likely causes, one quick fix + one strategic fix. Identify which stage improved by just 10% would have the biggest revenue impact.

Why It Works: The “10% improvement” calculation focuses attention on the highest-leverage stage.

Impact: Improving sign-up‚Üífirst-action by 10% adds 34 paid conversions/month. Onboarding friction ‚Äî not acquisition ‚Äî is your growth constraint.

7.2 ‚Äî CRO Audit Checklist

Generate a CRO audit for [page/flow]. Organise by: above the fold, trust elements, friction points, copy, mobile experience, psychology. Rate each: critical/important/nice-to-have. Provide the specific fix, not just the diagnosis.

Why It Works: Prioritised items with specific fixes prevent vague “improve the headline” recommendations.

Critical: Form asks for company size before showing value. Reduce from 6 to 3 fields ‚Äî typically increases completion 25-40%.

7.3 ‚Äî Growth Experiment Designer

Design 5 growth experiments for [product] targeting [metric]. Each: hypothesis, experiment design, success metric, minimum viable test, timeline, kill criteria. Prioritise by ICE score (Impact √ó Confidence √ó Ease).

Why It Works: Kill criteria and minimum viable tests prevent the two most common failures: running too long and over-building.

MVP test: Manually email 50 inactive signups with a personalised nudge. Track completion rate before building automation.

7.4 ‚Äî User Journey Mapper

Map the journey for [persona] from awareness through advocacy. Each stage: what they think/feel, actions taken, channels involved, friction risks, ideal experience, one improvement. Include emotional state markers (frustrated/curious/confident/anxious).

Why It Works: Extending past purchase through retention reveals where the highest-value optimisation lives.

Decision stage ‚Äî anxious: No case study matching their industry. They leave to “think about it” and never return.

7.5 ‚Äî Pricing Page Optimisation

Critique our pricing page: [describe tiers, prices, features, CTAs]. Analyse: tier clarity (can visitors choose in 10 seconds?), anchoring, feature ordering, missing elements, and rewrite tier names/descriptions/CTAs for conversion.

Why It Works: Pricing pages are the highest-intent page on most sites yet often designed by product teams, not conversion experts.

All tiers use “Get Started.” Rewrite: Free ‚Üí “Start Free”, Growth ‚Üí “Start Growing”, Enterprise ‚Üí “Talk to Us.”

Chapter 088 ¬∑ Bonus: Campaign Planning Workflow

Run these five prompts in sequence ‚Äî each builds on the previous output.

Step 1 ‚Äî Strategy Brief

Planning a campaign for [product/service], objective: [describe]. Define: measurable goal, target personas, key message (one sentence), success metrics, budget allocation framework (% split), timeline with milestones, single biggest risk and mitigation.

Step 2 ‚Äî Channel Plan

Based on the strategy from Step 1, create a channel plan. Per channel: role (awareness/consideration/conversion), content formats needed, budget %, expected KPI contribution, one channel-specific tactic that beats generic execution.

Step 3 ‚Äî Content & Creative Brief

From the channel plan, create a production brief. Per content piece: format/platform, message/CTA, copy length/tone, visual direction for designers, production timeline. Group by dependency ‚Äî parallel vs sequential work.

Step 4 ‚Äî Launch Sequence

Create a 14-day launch sequence. Per day: what goes live, what to monitor, decision points ("If [metric] below [threshold] by Day [X], then [action]"). Include a Day -3 pre-launch checklist.

Step 5 ‚Äî Measurement Plan

Design the measurement plan. Include: dashboard structure, reporting cadence (daily launch week, weekly after), attribution approach, post-mortem template, and 3 early-warning signals with specific pivots.

Why the workflow works: Five sequential prompts produce a campaign plan that would typically take a senior marketer 2-3 days. Each prompt references the previous output, creating a coherent strategy rather than disconnected documents.
